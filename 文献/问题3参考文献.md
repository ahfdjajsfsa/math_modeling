Abstract
Keywords: reference compound selection; multi-objective optimization; toxicity assay
validation; cheminformatics; design space
In chemical safety assessment, validation studies rely on reference compound
lists to evaluate the applicability of alternative methods prior to regulatory
acceptance. These lists are expected to cover multiple aspects, including
chemical structure, physicochemical properties, and toxicity profiles. In
practice, however, trade-offs among these aspects are typically addressed
implicitly through expert judgment, making them difficult to examine
systematically. Here, we formulate reference compound selection for toxicity
assay validation as an explicit multi-objective design problem. We define three
interpretable objectives capturing structural, physicochemical, and toxicity
diversity, and employ a genetic algorithm as an exploratory tool to examine the
trade-off structure and resulting Pareto-optimal solutions. Rather than
prescribing optimal or recommended compound sets, this formulation enables
systematic exploration of designs and explicit comparison of their positions
within a common design space. As an illustrative application, we link existing
assay datasets with expert-curated validation lists by treating “selected as a
reference compound” as an annotation on the underlying compound pool. We
show that expert-selected, random, and algorithmically generated compound
lists occupy distinct regions of the design space. Furthermore, under an
illustrative fixed modeling setup, different regions of the design space were
associated with different observed evaluation outcomes, supporting the view
that reference compound selection constitutes a structured dimension of
evaluation design. Together, these results provide a methodological perspective
for treating reference compound selection as an analyzable design object,
complementing established expert-driven practices.

1 Introduction
In chemical safety assessment, the evaluation of potential hazards posed by chemical
substances is essential1
. Animal testing has long played a central role in safety assessment;
however, ethical concerns, high costs, long testing periods, and interspecies variability have
motivated the development of alternative approaches2,3
. In response, a range of alternative
safety assessment methods, including in silico prediction models4,5
and in vitro toxicity
assays6–8
, have attracted increasing attention.
Before regulatory acceptance, these methods must undergo validation to assess their
predictive performance, reliability, and reproducibility9–11
. Validation studies of toxicity
assays typically rely on carefully curated reference compound lists, which are designed to
cover appropriate ranges of toxicity potency, chemical structure, and physicochemical
properties10
. These reference compounds are generally selected by domain experts,
informed by historical precedent, experimental feasibility, and available toxicity databases,
often in alignment with regulatory frameworks such as the Globally Harmonized System of
Classification and Labelling of Chemicals (GHS)12,13
.
Despite their central role, reference compound lists are rarely treated as explicit objects
of methodological design. Coverage along one dimension—for example, toxicity potency—
may constrain or trade off against coverage along others, such as structural or
physicochemical diversity. In practice, these trade-offs are addressed through expert
judgment, which is both necessary and effective, but the resulting trade-off structure
remains implicit, making it difficult to analyze and compare systematically. For example,
in assay-specific toxicity validation, emphasizing diversity in toxicological responses may
narrow the range of chemical scaffolds represented in the set, whereas prioritizing broad
structural coverage can introduce compounds that are less directly aligned with the assay
context or endpoint-specific relevance criteria.
From a methodological perspective, reference compound selection can be viewed as a
design problem defined under multiple, potentially conflicting objectives. Different
selections can occupy distinct positions within the design space, even when the size of the
compound list and the validation protocol are held fixed. Making these trade-offs explicit
provides a transparent analytical basis for discussing how design choices shape validation
datasets.
In this study, we formulate reference compound selection for validation of toxicity assays
as a multi-objective trade-off problem. Rather than prescribing optimal compound sets, we
use a genetic algorithm (GA) as an exploratory tool to examine the structure of this trade-
off space14
. GA is well suited for this purpose because it can efficiently explore complex
combinatorial spaces and identify Pareto-optimal solutions, where improvement along one
objective necessarily involves compromise along others15
. The primary contribution of this
study lies not in proposing a new optimization algorithm or a preferred reference list, but
in formalizing reference compound selection as an explicit and comparable design
component in toxicity assay validation. Accordingly, the present study should be read as a
methodological case study on validation design, rather than as a benchmark comparison of
predictive models or a prescriptive framework for selecting recommended compound sets.
2 Related Work
2.1 Multi-Objective Optimization and Genetic Algorithms
Multi-objective optimization provides a natural framework for problems in which several
desirable properties must be considered simultaneously under inherent trade-offs. In such
settings, improvement in one objective often requires compromise in another, making
Pareto-optimal solutions a useful representation of achievable design alternatives. Genetic
algorithms (GA) have been widely used to explore such trade-off structures in complex
combinatorial spaces, particularly when objective functions are nonlinear or

discontinuous15–18
.
In the present study, GA is not introduced as a methodological contribution in itself.
Rather, it is used as an exploratory tool for examining feasible regions of the design space
and visualizing the Pareto structure induced by multiple design objectives. This role is
consistent with the aim of the study, which is to formalize reference compound selection as
an analyzable design problem rather than to propose a new optimization algorithm or to
identify a uniquely preferred solution.
2.2 Evaluation Design and Dataset Construction in Cheminformatics and
Computational Toxicology
In cheminformatics and computational toxicology, evaluation outcomes depend strongly on
how datasets are constructed and partitioned. Prior studies have shown that commonly used
random-split validation can yield results that differ substantially from those obtained under
more application-oriented settings, such as time-based splits, which better reflect temporal
distribution shifts encountered in practice19,20
. This line of work has established evaluation
design as an important methodological issue in predictive modeling.
A representative example is Simulated Medicinal Chemistry Project Data (SIMPD),
which uses a multi-objective GA framework to generate training–test splits that emulate
temporal progression in medicinal chemistry projects21
. Such studies address how data
partitioning affects the realism of model evaluation22
. The present study is related in spirit
but addresses a different question. Rather than focusing on how an existing dataset should
be split, we examine how the internal composition of a reference compound set can itself
be treated as an explicit design variable in validation studies. In this sense, reference
compound selection and data-splitting strategies operate at different but complementary
levels of evaluation design.
This distinction is particularly relevant in toxicity assay validation, where reference
compound lists are often expert-curated and assay-specific, yet the trade-off structure
underlying their composition is rarely made explicit. Our work therefore builds on prior
evaluation design studies while shifting attention from partition strategy to reference-set
composition as a comparable object of methodological analysis.
3 Methods
3.1 Validation Studies and Data Sources
3.1.1 Validation Studies
The present study analyzed reference compound lists officially used in validation studies
conducted by the Japanese Center for the Validation of Alternative Methods (JaCVAM)23
as
case-study datasets for methodological exploration. These validation studies cover acute,
endocrine, and developmental toxicity assays that have been internationally evaluated and
are aligned with corresponding OECD Test Guidelines.
The selected assays span multiple toxicological endpoints and application contexts,
reflecting the diversity of chemical safety assessment practices rather than a focus on a
specific regulatory domain or chemical class. In particular, for endocrine disruption,
multiple assays targeting estrogen and androgen receptor signaling—VM7Luc ER TA, ER
STTA, AR-EcoScreen, AR-CALUX, and hrER binding—were included to capture distinct
endpoints such as receptor transactivation and ligand binding.
Full assay specifications, numbers of reference compounds, and links to external toxicity
databases are provided in Table 1 and Supplementary Table 1. In this study, these
validation studies are treated as fixed design contexts, and no attempt is made to reinterpret
their regulatory conclusions or experimental outcomes.

3.1.2 Large-Scale Toxicity Databases
To link the reference compounds from JaCVAM validation studies with publicly available
toxicity information, three large-scale databases— ICE24
, TDC25
, and Tox2126
—were
employed (see Table 1 and Supplementary Table 1). Each database covers complementary
aspects of chemical and biological variation, allowing consistent extraction of
physicochemical and toxicity data across assays.
The Integrated Chemical Environment (ICE), developed by the National Toxicology
Program Interagency Center for the Evaluation of Alternative Toxicological Methods
(NICEATM, U.S. NTP), provides curated datasets integrating in vitro, in vivo, and in silico
toxicity data. In this study, ICE served as the primary source for acute oral toxicity and
developmental toxicity information. Acute toxicity data included quantitative measures
such as LD₅₀ values, whereas developmental toxicity data were obtained from the DART
(Developmental and Reproductive Toxicology) dataset, which includes endpoints such as
differentiation inhibition indices.
The Therapeutics Data Commons (TDC) is an open-source platform integrating multiple
biomedical resources, including Tox2126
, SIDER27
, and ChEMBL. In this study, TDC was
used as the primary access point for endocrine disruption–related assays, including VM7Luc
ER TA, ER STTA, AR-EcoScreen, AR-CALUX, and hrER binding. For each compound,
corresponding assay endpoints—such as receptor transactivation activity or binding
affinity—were retrieved using standardized SMILES-based identifiers provided through the
TDC interface.
Tox21 is a joint initiative of the U.S. EPA, NIH, and FDA that provides high-throughput
screening (HTS) data aimed at reducing reliance on traditional animal testing. In this study,
Tox21 data were accessed via the TDC interface to supplement receptor-related assay
endpoints for estrogen and androgen receptor signaling. The broad assay coverage of Tox21
enabled inclusion of compounds spanning diverse biological response profiles, supporting
systematic exploration of reference compound selection across multiple assay contexts.
For each compound included in the JaCVAM validation studies listed in Table 1, the
corresponding CAS Registry Number (CAS-RN) was used to retrieve the canonical SMILES
representation via the PubChem API28
. All SMILES were standardized and converted into
isomeric SMILES using RDKit29
(version 2024.09.5), ensuring consistent structural
encoding across databases.
Using these standardized identifiers, compounds were matched to entries in the databases
described above, thereby defining a candidate compound pool for each validation study.
Acute and developmental toxicity assays were primarily linked to ICE datasets, whereas
endocrine disruption assays were mapped to corresponding endpoints available through
TDC and Tox21.
When an isomeric SMILES match was available, toxicity data were adopted directly from
the database. When database entries were unavailable, toxicity information was extracted
from the original JaCVAM validation documents (Supplementary Table 1). Differences in
experimental protocols, endpoint definitions, and reporting conventions across assays and
databases were not reconciled. Accordingly, toxicity values and labels were treated as assay-
specific descriptors rather than as directly comparable endpoints across assays.
For regression-type endpoints (e.g., LD₅₀), numerical values were normalized to unit
variance. For classification-type endpoints (e.g., agonist versus antagonist), binary labels
followed the conventions of the source database. These data integration and preprocessing
steps were applied uniformly across all analyses.
3.2 Problem Formulation
3.2.1 Reference Compound Selection as a Design Problem
In this study, reference compound selection for toxicity assay validation is formulated as an


## 第 5 页

explicit design problem. Given a fixed validation context—defined by assay type, endpoint
definition, and compound list size—the task is to select a subset of compounds from a larger
candidate compound pool such that multiple, potentially conflicting design objectives are
jointly considered.
Importantly, this formulation does not aim to identify an optimal or recommended
reference compound list. Instead, it provides a structured formulation for exploring how
different design choices position compound lists within the design space defined by the
specified objectives. Expert-driven reference lists used in existing validation studies are
therefore treated as one realization within this design space, rather than as a ground truth
or target solution.
3.2.2 Decision Variables and Constraints
The decision variable in this formulation is the composition of a reference compound list of
fixed size , selected from a candidate compound pool of compounds associated with a
given validation study. The list size is constrained to match that of the corresponding
JaCVAM validation study to enable direct comparison under consistent experimental
contexts.
Additional constraints are imposed implicitly by data availability: only compounds for
which chemical structures and assay-specific toxicity information can be retrieved from
public databases or original validation documents are considered. No constraints related to
compound cost, availability, or experimental feasibility are included, as such information is
not consistently available in structured form.
3.2.3 Objective Functions
To explore the design space of reference compound selection, three objective functions were
defined to capture complementary aspects that are commonly considered in the construction
of reference compound lists for toxicity assay validation: chemical structure diversity,
physicochemical property diversity, and toxicity diversity.
These objectives do not represent regulatory definitions or exhaustive criteria for
reference compound selection. Rather, they are operational proxies introduced to enable
systematic and transparent exploration of trade-offs under explicit and controlled
assumptions. Alternative formulations or additional objectives could be adopted depending
on the specific validation context; the present study deliberately restricts the objectives to
these three dimensions to maintain interpretability and computational tractability.
Chemical structure diversity reflects the extent to which compounds differ in their
molecular scaffolds and substructures, and is commonly emphasized to ensure broad
coverage of chemical space. Physicochemical property diversity captures variation in
properties relevant to assay behavior and compound disposition, such as lipophilicity and
polarity. Toxicity diversity represents variation in assay-specific response profiles, such as
potency ranges or class balance, which is important to avoid overrepresentation of narrow
response regimes in validation datasets.
For all objectives, diversity was quantified at the level of compound pairs within each
reference compound list. The objective functions were formulated such that lower overall
similarity or greater dispersion corresponds to higher diversity. These formulations allow
different reference compound lists of fixed size to be compared within a common multi-
dimensional objective space, enabling explicit visualization of trade-offs among competing
design criteria.
The specific mathematical definitions of each objective function are provided in the
following sections. Importantly, the resulting objective values should be interpreted as
relative indicators within the defined design space, rather than as absolute measures of
reference compound quality.

3.3 Multi-Objective Optimization Framework
3.3.1 Genetic Algorithm Implementation
To explore the multi-objective trade-off structure defined in Section 3.2, we employed a GA
as an exploratory computational approach. GA was chosen not to identify an optimal or
recommended reference compound list, but to efficiently sample and visualize feasible
regions of the design space under multiple competing objectives.
The optimization was implemented using a standard NSGA-II (Non-dominated Sorting
Genetic Algorithm II)30
–based multi-objective genetic algorithm. Candidate reference
compound lists of fixed size were represented as individuals, and evolutionary operations
including selection, crossover, and mutation were applied iteratively to generate diverse
compound lists spanning the objective space.
At each generation, individuals were evaluated according to the defined objective
functions, and non-dominated sorting was used to identify Pareto-optimal solutions.
Crowding distance was employed to maintain diversity along the Pareto front, ensuring
coverage of distinct regions of the design space rather than convergence to a single solution.
The final output of the optimization is the Pareto front, representing the boundary of
feasible reference compound list designs under the specified objectives and constraints.
Importantly, the structure of the Pareto front is independent of any weighting among
objectives. All subsequent analyses therefore refer explicitly to the distribution and
structure of solutions on the Pareto front, rather than to a single optimized list.
Implementation details, including population size, number of generations, crossover and
mutation rates, and software versions, are provided in the Supplementary Notes.

Toxicity diversity was evaluated in an assay-specific manner. For continuous toxicity
endpoints (e.g., LD₅₀), diversity was quantified using the coefficient of variation of log-
transformed toxicity values within the list. For binary endpoints (e.g., toxic versus non-
toxic, agonist versus antagonist), diversity was quantified using the log-likelihood of a
binomial distribution with fixed success probability of 0.5, which assigns higher scores
to more balanced class compositions. Because toxicity endpoints differ across assays in
scale and type (continuous or binary), the corresponding mathematical definitions are
provided in the Supplementary Notes.
These metrics are not intended as definitive or regulatory measures of diversity.
Rather, they serve as interpretable operational proxies that enable systematic
comparison of reference compound lists within a common multi-objective design space.
Additional details regarding metric definitions, normalization procedures, and
alternative formulations are provided in the Supplementary Notes.
3.4 Illustrative Evaluation Analyses
To illustrate how reference compound selection shapes downstream evaluation
characteristics, we conducted two complementary analyses based on the compound lists
generated by the multi-objective optimization. These analyses are intended as illustrative
assessments of the implications of different design choices, rather than as benchmarks for
comparing predictive models. For visualization and illustrative comparison purposes only,
a single illustrative Pareto-optimal example was selected from the Pareto front using a
composite score; details are provided in the Supplementary Notes.
First, compound lists generated by the GA were compared with randomly generated lists
and the original reference lists used in validation studies using the same objectives defined
in Section 3.3.2. This comparison characterizes how different selection strategies position
reference compound lists within the multi-objective design space defined by structural,
physicochemical, and toxicity diversity.
Second, evaluation outcomes were examined using a representative machine learning
model for toxicity prediction. For each reference compound list, models were trained on the
remaining compounds and evaluated on the selected list. Differences in predictive
performance metrics (e.g., accuracy and AUC) were interpreted as reflecting differences in
test set composition and heterogeneity, rather than intrinsic differences in model capability.
These analyses do not aim to establish the superiority, rigor, or preference of any
particular evaluation setup. Instead, they are intended to show that, under otherwise
comparable modeling conditions, reference compound selection constitutes a structured
dimension of evaluation design and contributes to variation in observed evaluation
outcomes. Implementation details of the modeling and evaluation procedures are provided
in the Supplementary Notes.

4 Results

endocrine disruption screening35

4.1 Analysis of Reference Compound Properties Used in Validation Studies
We first examined the properties of reference compound lists used in existing validation
studies. These lists are selected by domain experts and reflect practical considerations
related to assay purpose, feasibility, and interpretability within specific validation contexts.
Figure 1 compares the structural, physicochemical, and toxicity diversity metrics of the
ER-STTA reference compound list with distributions obtained from 10,000 randomly
generated compound lists sampled from the corresponding toxicity dataset.
Relative to randomly generated selections, the ER-STTA reference compound list shows
lower structural diversity, corresponding to higher internal structural similarity under the
present metric definition. This result suggests that, for this assay, the selected compounds
occupy a relatively narrower region of structural space than randomly generated lists.
Across other validation assays, however, the relative position of the reference list with
respect to the random distributions is assay-dependent rather than uniform, as shown in
Supplementary Figure S1. Representative molecular structures from these reference lists
are provided in Figure 1D and Supplementary Figure S2.
4.2 Genetic Algorithm Optimization Process
To examine the behavior of the GA during optimization, we tracked the evolution of
diversity metrics across generations. Figure 2 shows the progression of population-
averaged structural, physicochemical, and toxicity diversity scores over the course of the
evolutionary process. Across all objectives, the distributions stabilized over successive
generations, consistent with convergence of the search process.
Figure 3 visualizes the distribution of compound lists in the objective space at the initial
and final generations. Compared with the initial random population, compound lists in the
final generation occupy distinct regions of the design space, with part of the final population
distributed along the Pareto front. A single illustrative Pareto-optimal example was selected
from the final Pareto front using the composite score described in the Supplementary Notes
and used only for illustrative comparisons. Comparable convergence behavior and Pareto
front evolution for other assays are shown in Supplementary Figures S3 and S4.
4.3 Comparison of GA-Derived and Validation Reference Compound Lists
We next compared compound lists generated by the genetic algorithm with the original
reference compound lists used in validation studies, as well as with randomly generated
compound lists. As shown in Figures 4A–4C, GA-derived compound lists occupy regions
of the multi-objective design space that differ from those corresponding to both randomly
generated and expert-selected reference lists, exhibiting distinct combinations of structural,
physicochemical, and toxicity diversity scores.
Visual inspection of an illustrative GA-derived compound list (Figure 4D) highlights
differences in chemical composition relative to the original validation reference list. Similar
distributional patterns across additional validation assays are presented in Supplementary
Figure S5. These observations support the view that systematic exploration of the multi-
objective design space can identify reference compound lists with diversity profiles that
differ from those obtained through manual selection or random sampling, without implying
preference or superiority among these selections. This illustrative Pareto-optimal example
is shown solely to visualize one feasible composition within the trade-off space and is not
interpreted as a preferred, recommended, or uniquely meaningful solution.

4.4 Illustrative Evaluation Outcomes Associated with Compound List Selection
We next examined how differences in reference compound composition were reflected
in observed evaluation outcomes when the same modeling procedure was applied across
test sets. To this end, toxicity prediction models were trained under a common protocol,
and three types of compound lists were used as held-out test sets: Pareto-front lists
identified by the genetic algorithm, intermediate lists sampled during the optimization
process, and randomly generated lists.
As shown in Figure 5, evaluation metrics differed across these test sets under
otherwise comparable training settings. Similar patterns were also observed in other
assays (Supplementary Figure S6). These differences indicate that reference compound
lists occupying different regions of the multi-objective design space correspond to
distinct evaluation outcome profiles. Notably, compound lists located closer to the
Pareto front showed evaluation outcome profiles that differed from those obtained with
randomly generated lists under the same modeling procedure.
These observations should not be interpreted as evidence that Pareto-front lists
provide a more rigorous, superior, or preferred evaluation. Rather, they show that
observed evaluation outcomes vary with design choices in reference compound selection
even when the modeling procedure is held fixed.
5 Discussion
In this study, we formulated reference compound selection for toxicity assay validation
as an explicit multi-objective design problem. Rather than aiming to improve predictive
models or establish more stringent evaluation benchmarks, our primary contribution lies
in the formulation itself, which makes the trade-offs underlying reference compound
selection explicit and suitable for systematic analysis under controlled assumptions.
A central implication of this formulation is that differences in reference compound
selection can be situated and compared as positions within an explicit multi-objective
design space, even when the modeling approach and training protocol are held fixed.
This makes it possible to relate differences in observed evaluation outcomes to specific
regions of the trade-off space in test-set composition, rather than treating them as
isolated or ad hoc effects. In this sense, the present study should be understood as a
methodological case study on evaluation design across several validation contexts,
rather than as a comprehensive survey of all toxicity assay types or a benchmark
comparison of predictive models.
This perspective complements prior evaluation design studies that have focused
primarily on data-splitting strategies such as random or time-based splits19–21
. Rather
than replacing such protocols, the present formulation addresses a different but
complementary question: how the internal composition of reference compound sets
structures the evaluation setting itself. For example, emphasizing diversity in
toxicological responses may narrow the range of chemical scaffolds represented in a set,
whereas prioritizing broad structural coverage can introduce compounds that are less
directly aligned with the assay context or endpoint-specific relevance criteria. Reference
compound selection and data-splitting strategies therefore operate at different but
complementary levels of evaluation design and can be considered jointly in future
studies.
Several limitations follow directly from the assumptions made. The objectives
considered here capture only three commonly invoked aspects of reference compound
selection and do not reflect regulatory definitions or expert judgment in full. In addition,
factors such as compound cost, availability, and experimental feasibility were not

included because consistent quantitative descriptors were not available. These
limitations do not undermine the formulation itself; rather, they define its current scope
and clarify which aspects of reference compound selection are being modeled explicitly.
The evaluation analyses raise a distinct interpretive issue. They are not intended to
establish that any particular reference compound list provides a more rigorous, superior,
or preferred evaluation. Nor do they isolate the effect of test-set composition from all
sources of model variability. However, because the same model family, tuning procedure,
and evaluation protocol were used throughout, the results support the view that reference
compound selection is a non-negligible design factor contributing to variation in
observed evaluation outcomes under otherwise comparable modeling conditions. Here,
assay-dependent should be understood operationally, as reflecting differences in
candidate pools, endpoint definitions, and selection contexts, rather than implying a
uniquely identifiable mechanistic explanation.
Overall, this work provides a methodological perspective for treating reference
compound selection as an explicit design component in toxicity assay validation that
builds on established expert-driven practices. By making underlying trade-offs
explicitly comparable within a common design space, the formulation offers a
transparent basis for discussing evaluation design choices and their consequences. More
broadly, the present results suggest that the structure of the design space is assay-
dependent rather than universal, providing a way to examine trade-offs that may
otherwise remain implicit in expert-driven selection.

Supplementary Notes
Note S1. Methodological Limitations and Future Extensions
Several practical factors and modeling choices were not incorporated into the current
formulation. While these simplifications enabled direct comparison with existing validation
studies under controlled assumptions, they also define the present scope of the analysis.
Size of Compound Lists
In this study, compound list size was fixed to match that used in each original validation
assay, ensuring a consistent basis for comparison within each validation context. However,
this constraint prevents exploration of formulations in which list size itself is treated as a
design variable.
Allowing variable list sizes could support analysis of more complex design questions,
such as how coverage of chemical space, assay relevance, and experimental feasibility trade
off against one another. Future extensions could therefore include list size as an additional
decision variable and examine how it alters the structure of the design space.
Cost and Experimental Feasibility
Factors such as compound cost, handling feasibility, and inter-laboratory reproducibility are
important in actual validation studies but were not included here because consistent
quantitative descriptors were not available in structured form.
Validation assays often prioritize compounds that are stable, easy to handle, and likely to
result in reproducible measurements across laboratories. Although these factors are relevant
to practical implementation, they are rarely represented in a form that can be incorporated
directly into the present formulation. If such metadata become available in the future, they
could be introduced as additional objectives or constraints, thereby extending the realism
of the design space without changing the basic formulation presented here.
Note S2. Genetic Algorithm Implementation Details
Reference compound selection was explored using a multi-objective genetic algorithm (GA)
implemented with the DEAP Python library (version 1.4.2)37
. The NSGA-II (Non-dominated
Sorting Genetic Algorithm II)30
framework was adopted to maintain a diverse set of non-
dominated solutions in the design space.
Each candidate reference compound list was represented as an individual consisting of a
fixed number of compounds, matching the size of the corresponding JaCVAM validation
study. The initial population consisted of 100 compound lists randomly sampled from the
candidate compound pool defined for each validation context.
Evolutionary operators included crossover and mutation. Crossover exchanged subsets of
compounds between two parent lists, whereas mutation replaced one or more compounds
with alternatives drawn from the remaining candidate pool. The crossover rate and mutation
rate were set to 0.8 and 0.2, respectively.
Selection was performed using the NSGA-II procedure. At each generation, individuals
were ranked by Pareto dominance, and crowding distance was used to promote diversity
along the Pareto front. The evolutionary process was repeated for 1,000 generations. At the
final generation, the Pareto front was extracted using the tools.ParetoFront() function
implemented in DEAP.
The GA parameters, including population size, number of generations, crossover rate, and
mutation rate, were not optimized for performance. Instead, standard values commonly used
in NSGA-II–based exploratory analyses were adopted to support stable exploration and
reproducible sampling of the design space. Accordingly, these parameters were treated as
fixed implementation choices rather than variables of interest in the present study.

Note S5. Evaluation Procedures for Illustrative Analyses
Evaluation Based on Objective Function Scores
Compound lists generated by the genetic algorithm were compared with randomly
generated compound lists and with the original reference lists used in validation studies.
Comparisons were conducted using the same three diversity metrics employed during
optimization: structural diversity, physicochemical property diversity, and toxicity
diversity. These comparisons characterize how different selection strategies are
distributed within the multi-objective design space.
Evaluation of Model Performance Characteristics
To examine illustrative evaluation outcomes, XGBoost38
models (version 2.1.4) were
trained on datasets excluding the compounds in each test list and evaluated on the
corresponding held-out compounds. For each training–test split, model hyperparameters
were optimized using the same Optuna-based procedure to ensure consistent model fitting
across different compound lists. XGBoost was selected as a representative and widely used
machine learning method for toxicity prediction on tabular chemical descriptors, serving
here as a stable evaluation probe rather than as a subject of model comparison.
Hyperparameter optimization was performed using Optuna over the following parameters:
max_depth (3–12), learning_rate (0.01–0.2), n_estimators (50–1000), subsample (0.6–1.0),
colsample_bytree (0.6–1.0), gamma (0–1), and min_child_weight (1–10), with the objective
function fixed to squared error regression.
Prediction metrics such as accuracy and AUC were compared across test sets. Because
each test set consists of a distinct set of compounds, differences in these metrics were
interpreted as reflecting variation associated with test-set composition under a common
modeling procedure, while not excluding contributions from model fitting variability or
limited test-set size. Accordingly, these analyses are not intended to isolate pure test-set
effects or to compare modeling strategies, but to examine whether observed evaluation
outcomes vary systematically with reference compound selection under otherwise
comparable conditions.
